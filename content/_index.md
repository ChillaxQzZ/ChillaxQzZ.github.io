Three-dimensional medical imaging reveals the complexity of human anatomical and pathological structures with unprecedented precision, while cutting-edge large AI models are reshaping how we extract diagnostic evidence from massive visual data. However, bridging the dimensional gap for these models to efficiently and accurately understand complex 3D spatial structures remains a fundamental challenge. My research lies at the intersection of Biomedical Engineering and Artificial Intelligence, utilizing spatial algorithms and dimensionality reduction reconstruction technologies to explore the 2D visual representation of complex medical data.

## Current Research

### Spatial Modeling and Feature Extraction of Cardiovascular Pathology
I utilize advanced geometric computational strategies, such as Signed Distance Functions (SDF), to integrate complex 3D cardiovascular imaging data. Through precise field modeling, I aim to track deep clues hidden within complex vascular topologies, enabling accurate anchoring and isolation of critical lesions such as thrombi and blood stasis.

### Medical Visual Data Adaptation for Large Models
I develop computational pipelines bridging dimensions—encompassing 3D surface reconstruction and optimized 2D projection mapping. These are specifically designed to losslessly and clearly transform high-dimensional spatial pathology information into 2D feature atlases that are easily parsed by Large Language Models (LLMs) and Large Vision Models (LVMs).

### Structured Management of Medical Imaging Feature Libraries
I leverage advanced database architectures and complex SQL querying techniques to integrate multimodal medical imaging metadata with 2D feature labels generated post-dimensionality reduction. By systematically managing and analyzing the latent correlations within massive datasets, I provide a stable, efficient, and structured data foundation for subsequent AI deep learning and model validation.
